Your Grill May Have Free Replacement Parts Waiting

Firefly Propane Team Updated 2026 4 min read

Most grill owners assume that when a part breaks or wears out, it's time to open their wallet. A new burner, a replacement igniter, a fresh set of grates — it adds up fast. But here's something a lot of people don't know: your grill manufacturer may already owe you those parts, completely free of charge. The warranty coverage you paid for when you bought your grill might still be active, and you may never have used it once.

What is a grill warranty, and what does it cover?

When you purchase a gas grill, it almost always comes with a manufacturer's warranty — though the coverage length and terms vary widely by brand and model. Most warranties fall into a few categories:

  • Burner warranties — often 5 to 10 years, sometimes lifetime on premium grills
  • Cooking grate warranties — typically 2 to 5 years against rust-through or defects
  • Igniter warranties — usually 1 to 3 years
  • Housing and lid warranties — often 3 to 5 years against paint peeling or rust-through
  • Electrical component warranties — varies by manufacturer

Brands like Weber, Napoleon, Broil King, and DCS are well known for honoring warranty claims — but only if you file one. The manufacturer won't come looking for you. If you don't know the process, those benefits quietly expire unused.

How do you know if your grill is still under warranty?

The honest answer: most people have no idea. Warranties aren't prominently displayed on the grill itself, and the documentation that came in the box has usually long since been thrown away or buried in a drawer. Here's how to check:

  1. Find your model number It's usually on a sticker inside the lid, on the back of the grill, or on the cart frame. It looks something like "E-310" or "700 Series." This is what the manufacturer needs to look up your warranty terms.
  2. Check your purchase date A receipt, credit card statement, or online order confirmation is enough. If you registered your grill at purchase, even better — the manufacturer will have it on file.
  3. Visit the manufacturer's website Most major grill brands have a warranty lookup page or a customer service line where you can confirm coverage with your model number and purchase date.
  4. Call Firefly If this sounds like more work than you want to do — just call us. When we come out to service your grill, we assess its condition and check whether any failing or worn parts fall under the manufacturer's coverage. We do this as part of every service visit.

What parts are most commonly covered?

During a service visit, the components we most frequently find eligible for warranty replacement are:

  • Burner tubes that have corroded, warped, or developed uneven flame distribution
  • Cooking grates with rust-through or structural defects (not cosmetic surface rust from lack of maintenance)
  • Igniters that have failed entirely or only spark intermittently
  • Flavorizer bars or heat shields that have corroded beyond normal wear
  • Lid components with paint peeling or structural failure within the warranty window

Warranty claims typically cover manufacturing defects and premature failure — not damage from misuse or lack of maintenance. A clean, regularly serviced grill is more likely to have a valid claim honored.

Why does this matter?

Because a grill with a failing burner or a cracked grate isn't just annoying — it's a safety issue. Uneven heat distribution from a warped burner can cause dangerous flare-ups. A corroded grate can contaminate food. An igniter that doesn't spark reliably means you're letting gas accumulate before it lights — which is exactly the kind of thing that causes accidents.

Getting these parts replaced under warranty means your grill is restored to the condition it was designed to operate at — safely, efficiently, and without coming out of your pocket.
